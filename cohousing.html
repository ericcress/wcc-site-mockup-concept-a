What is Cohousing?

Independent, but never alone.

Cohousing is a way of organizing forty households so that the daily logistics of life — meals, garden, maintenance, conflict — happen among people who know each other. You own your private home. You share a common house. Decisions are made by consensus. Nobody runs it for you; you run it together.

It's older than it sounds. The first cohousing communities formed in Denmark in the 1970s. Washington Commons is one of more than 170 built communities in the United States — California has more than any other state.

In Practice

What it actually feels like, day to day.

The unit is yours. Private kitchen, private bath, private living room. The door closes; you're alone if you want to be.

The common house is shared. 5,800 square feet at Washington Commons — a kitchen big enough to cook for the community, a dining room built for forty, guest rooms for visiting family, craft and exercise spaces, terrace and gardens. Anyone in the community can use any of it.

The cadence is voluntary. Some weeks you cook a common dinner; some weeks you eat one a neighbor cooked; some weeks you don't touch the common house at all. The Friday Snack & Schmooze runs without you. The garden gets watered whether you're there or not. The community holds itself.

The participation is voluntary. The connection is the point.

The Model

Six things, in our words.

Adapted from Kathryn McCamant and Charles Durrett's Creating Cohousing. This is the model. This is how we run it.

01

Residents designed it

Four design workshops with the architect in 2019. Members chose the orientation, the common-house priorities, the unit layouts. The community shaped the building before it was built.

02

Designed for daily interaction

Every home opens onto shared walkways. The mail comes through the common house. You see your neighbors on the way to your unit — by design, not by accident.

03

Private home, shared everything else

Each unit is a full condominium — yours, fully self-sufficient. The common house extends what your home can be: a kitchen you can cook for forty in, a guest room you don't have to give up your office for.

04

The residents run it

No outside management company. Members serve on committees, perform maintenance, prepare communal meals, and meet regularly to handle the things that come up. Professionals come in when the task calls for it.

05

Consensus, not hierarchy

Leadership emerges naturally. Groups use consensus decision-making — every voice matters, decisions move forward with full group consent. Voting is the rare backstop.

06

No shared economy

Washington Commons is not a commune. Each household has independent income and finances. The community generates no income for members.

Adapted from Creating Cohousing: Building Sustainable Communities by Kathryn McCamant & Charles Durrett — the canonical reference for the cohousing model in North America. McCamant Cohousing Solutions co-developed Washington Commons with UD+P.
Buying in

Your home plus a way of life.

When you buy a home at Washington Commons, you buy a private condominium — the same legal structure as any condo development. You also buy into the community: a membership in the HOA, full access to the common house and grounds, a voice in consensus governance, and a place at every meal you want to attend.

The price reflects both. A 2-bedroom corner home in West Sacramento at this build quality runs around $600,000 alone. Washington Commons asks $750,000 — and the difference is what we've described on this page.

Context

One of many.

California has more built cohousing communities than any other state. PDX Commons in Portland (also UD+P-developed) opened in 2016. Washington Commons opened on the same lineage. Around the country, cohousing has been covered by the New York Times, the Atlantic, and NPR — most of it framed as the older-adult counter-narrative to assisted living.
