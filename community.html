The Community

Forty households who chose to know each other.

Washington Commons is a small community on purpose. About forty households share 5,800 square feet of common space on the riverside in West Sacramento. The neighbors run it themselves — they make decisions together, share meals when someone offers to host, and tend the garden in shifts.

Most of what follows is what daily life looks like.

Days at Washington Commons

The calendar fills itself.

Mornings are quiet. The garden is busy before the heat lifts — Bea waters most days, Willa is out with the landscaping team, Lenore is between her own 36 garden beds and the community planters. By mid-morning, the common-house kitchen has someone in it making coffee. Often two or three someones.

The week threads through the common house. Friday Snack & Schmooze most weeks — drinks and conversation in the evening, often spilling onto the terrace. Sunday Movie Night runs year-round. Brunches and dinners alternate. A potluck might be a Tuesday or a Saturday — depends on who's hosting. Workshops happen when someone wants to teach one. The craft room has a steady set of regulars. The exercise space stays in use.

Days that aren't on the calendar are the more telling part. Someone is reading on the terrace. Someone else's grandkids are in the guest suite for the weekend. A neighbor walks by your front door on the way to the mailbox and stops to talk. Janet plays pickleball. Lydia walks M'Chaiah. Stu fixes a bike. Carol cooks ahead for Day of the Dead. It's the rhythm of a small village.

The Common House

5,800 square feet of shared space.

The kitchen and dining room

Big enough to cook for the community when someone wants to. Stove on one wall, prep on the other, the long table built for forty.

The terrace and gardens

Native plantings, raised beds, dog wash at the far end, bird walks Bea leads in spring. The terrace fills up when the heat lifts.

Guest rooms, craft, exercise

Two guest rooms for visiting grandkids — one queen-and-pull-out, one full-size. Craft room for the regulars. Exercise space, used.

The workshop, the bikes, the daily stuff

Bike storage and EV charging in the garage. A workshop for the household projects no one keeps in their own unit. The infrastructure that makes daily life simpler.

Who Lives Here

A few of the neighbors.

Forty households means forty stories. A handful, to start.

Bea

Connector · Bird-walk leader

Bea is a recovering lobbyist and a whitewater guide, recently moved from Flagstaff. She waters the garden most mornings, leads the spring bird walks, and is the first call for resales — including for Unit 204. She's the community's connector.

Willa

Landscape lead · Baker

Willa heads the landscaping team. She can name the 35 plant species on the property and most of the birds. She came to cohousing through a housing-development background and an obsession with cooking.

Anne

Civic gravity · Walker

Anne is the founding president of WALKSacramento and retired from the California Air Resources Board. The neighbors describe her as "the one who sees the big picture." She is also a baker.

Governance

The neighbors run the place.

Washington Commons uses consensus decision-making — a model where every voice matters and decisions only move forward with full group consent. Committees handle landscape, finance, kitchen, common-space stewardship. Maintenance is shared among neighbors; professionals come in when needed.

The Homeowners Association is the legal shell. The community is the substance. Participation is voluntary — but the active members of the HOA are the active members of the community, by design.
