About Cohousing & Community Living
What is cohousing, exactly?
Cohousing is forty private homes organized around a common house. You own your unit; you share the kitchen, dining room, guest rooms, craft and exercise spaces, gardens, terrace, and dog wash. The community makes decisions by consensus. It's a model that started in Denmark in the 1970s — more than 170 built communities exist in the US now, more in California than any other state. Washington Commons is one. Read the longer version on /cohousing.
Will I still have privacy? What if I'm worried about "community" feeling overwhelming?
Your unit is fully private — kitchen, bathroom, living room, hookups for your own washer/dryer. The door closes. Participation in community events is entirely voluntary — there's no obligation to show up to the potlucks, the movie nights, the meetings. Some residents are deeply involved; some keep more to themselves. The community runs the same either way. You set the dial.
Ownership & Housing
Do I own my home?
Yes. Each home at Washington Commons is a private condominium with its own deed. You own it the same way you'd own any condo. The HOA governs the shared spaces and the community's collective decisions; the unit itself is yours.
What's included in the HOA dues?
Dues cover building maintenance (exterior, common areas, landscaping, shared infrastructure), shared utilities for the common house, reserves for capital expenses, and the operating costs of community amenities. Current dues are in the sales packet — they're consistent with what you'd pay for an HOA at a high-quality condo development of this size.
Is the home for sale (Unit 204) the only one available?
Yes — Unit 204 is the last UDP-owned home at Washington Commons. Resale homes come up occasionally; Bea on the community side tracks those — her number is in the sales packet.
Community Life
What kinds of events and gatherings does the community have?
Friday Snack & Schmooze is a fixture — drinks and conversation most weeks. Sunday Movie Night runs year-round. Potluck brunches and dinners alternate; a potluck might happen on a Tuesday or a Saturday depending on who's hosting. Monthly birthday celebrations. Spontaneous things — dog nights on the terrace, a workshop someone wants to teach, a Wednesday picnic, a Day of the Dead celebration. The calendar fills itself.
How do communal meals work?
Mostly potluck-style and family-style. The community is mindful of allergies and dietary restrictions — people communicate openly. If you cook, you cook for the community sometimes; if you don't cook much, you eat well sometimes. (To borrow from one resident: "I don't cook much, but I do enjoy eating.") Nobody is required to host.
Are pets allowed?
Yes. Lydia walks M'Chaiah every morning. There's a dog wash on the terrace. Pets of all kinds are welcome.
Where do my grandkids stay when they visit?
The common house has two guest rooms — one with a queen and a pull-out twin, one with a full-size. Residents reserve them through the community calendar. Visiting family stays in their own wing rather than the living-room couch.
Governance & Conflict
How does conflict get handled?
The community uses a collaborative approach to conflict resolution, grounded in cooperation, respect, and mutual support. The HOA has a structure for it; the day-to-day reality is that small disagreements get worked through directly, and larger ones get worked through together. The premise is that disagreement is normal and the relationships are worth the work.
How does the HOA work? How much time will I have to invest?
The HOA uses consensus decision-making — every voice matters, decisions move forward with full group consent. Active participation is encouraged but not mandatory; you set your level. There are committees (landscape, finance, kitchen, common-space) — most residents serve on one or two. Plan on a few hours a month if you're typical; some residents do more, some do less.
Accessibility & Practical
Is Washington Commons accessible for people with mobility challenges?
Yes. The community is fully accessible — elevators, ramps where needed, accessible common-house bathrooms. We've thought about aging in place as a core design goal.
How are shared spaces cleaned and maintained?
Residents share daily upkeep. Professionals come in for the bigger jobs — deep cleaning, technical maintenance, anything outside the scope of a normal homeowner. It's a self-managed community in the spirit of the model.
